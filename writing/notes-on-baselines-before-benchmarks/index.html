Notes on Baselines Before Benchmarks

May 5, 2026 · 5 min read · Placeholder essay

Benchmarks are seductive because they promise narrative. A single number can imply momentum, competition, and progress. The problem is that benchmarks only mean something when the baseline beneath them is stable enough to deserve comparison.

Most dramatic improvements are built on small assumptions

If the data split changed, the seed was lucky, or the preprocessing drifted between runs, the benchmark story is mostly theater. Baseline work is the quieter discipline of proving that the ordinary parts of the system behave the way you think they do.

A useful baseline checklist

  • Can the default setup be rerun with similar results?
  • Is the evaluation script checking the right thing?
  • Are failures concentrated in one obvious slice of the data?
  • Would another person understand the comparison without extra context?

None of this is glamorous, but it protects the site from filling up with unsupported stories. The project pages can eventually celebrate improvement. The writing pages should first explain why the comparison is worth trusting.
